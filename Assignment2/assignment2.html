Assignment 2 - How has the weather changed over the past 100 years?

Temperatures measured on land and at sea for more than a century show that Earth's globally averaged surface temperature is rising. Since 1970, global surface temperature rose at an average rate of about 0.17°C per decade—more than twice as fast as the 0.07°C per decade increase observed for the entire period of recorded observations (1880-2015). The average global temperature for 2016 was 0.94°C above the 20th century average of 13.9°C, surpassing the previous record warmth of 2015 by 0.04°C. Climate Change: Global Temperature, LuAnn Dahlman, September 11, 2017

Copenhagen (Annual temperatures)

St. Petersburg (Annual temperatures)

For these 2 visualizations, we computed annual min, max and average temperatures to show how annual temperature ranges differed from year to year in Copenhagen and St. Petersburg.

As expected, we can see signs of global warming by observing the trendline of average annual temperatures computed by least-squares fit. In Copenhagen, the temperature between years 1880-2011 changed by approx. 2 °C. In St. Petersburg, the temperature between years 1880-2016 changed by approx. 3 °C.

Zurich (Seasons)

This visualization allows us to observe the temperature changes, between 1880 and 2016, according to the different seasons of the year.

From these different line charts we notice a very slight increase in temperature for the winter, autumn and spring seasons. Nevertheless we realize that it is for the winter season that global warming is the most visible. Indeed, we notice that the temperature has on average increased by about 2 °C until today. These different line charts of the evolution of the temperature during the seasons also allow us to realize that on average some years were more rigorous than others and that there is a consequent temperature difference between the summer months and winter while the temperatures of the spring and autumn months remain almost identical.

Copenhagen (Months)

The bonus pie charts above show the monthly temperatures over all years with provided data for the corresponding cities (Copenhagen, St. Petersburg and Zurich). The color corresponds to the mean temperatures in the respective month (using a color scheme similar to coolwarm). Hovering on a month toggles more information including the min and max temperatures for that month throughout all years, as well as the mean value. Most importantly, the hover box shows a trend, which is the difference between the temperature between the first and the last years available in the data set. Positive values indicate an increase in temperatures, while negative values - a decrease. It can be seen from the charts that an increase predominates for most of the months in all 3 cities, indicating once again that the global warming is a fact.

Question 3.3 (Martin Metaksov)

D3 is meant for visualizing complex data sets, with high dimensionality, allowing for visualizing multiple levels of details and interactively displaying information which is otherwise difficult to read. However, we do not believe this is the case with sets large in size, as there will always be a certain bottleneck reached when loading (e.g. due to cluttering the DOM, or other browser limitations). The larger the sets and the more the computational logic, the more the CPU will be loaded, thus the slower the interaction. At a certain point this can lead to unbearable delays (e.g. possibly files larger than 100MB will present an issue).

It is often the case that large data sets can undergo certain pre-processing, encoding, morton encoding and more. This could reduce the sizes and allow for less CPU operations later, which could significantly speedup the processing of large data sets. One example can be seen in www.idav.ucdavis.edu/func/return_pdf?pub_id=162. Furthermore, it is often possible to load only portions of the data at a time and visualize them only. As seen in http://polymaps.org/ex/flickr.html this provides a significant downside, as the data takes some time to load on each interaction, however can be displayed in a reasonable amount of time.

For this particular project, the sizes of the data sets has not been a problem as they were not larger than a few kilobytes. With this said, there was no reason to reduce their sizes.
